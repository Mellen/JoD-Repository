Decision Making Experiment

Instructions for participants

Thank you for participating in this study. The study will last approximately 15-20 minutes in total, while this first task will take only approximately 5 minutes. It is therefore very important that you read these instructions for the first task with care.

These instructions are solely for your private use. You should complete this study on your own, away from others.

You will be endowed with £10.00 in total through the study (£1.00 per round over 10 rounds) and paid at the end of the study. No other participant will know how much you leave with. How much you are paid depends on your decisions, the decisions of the participants you are paired with in each round, and chance, as determined by a dice roll. The dice roll is included to ‘hide’ your, and the other participants’, decisions from each other, as explained below.

The minimum you will leave with is £4.00. The maximum you can leave with is £10.00.

During each round of the study you will receive £1.00 of your £10.00 endowment. There are ten rounds in total, hence your total endowment is £10.00.

In the study you are randomly paired with a different anonymous participant in each round. You are never paired with the same participant twice. You will not learn the identity of any of the participants you are paired with in each round, and vice versa - the other participants will never learn your identity.

In each round you and your new partner both receive an endowment of 100 pence (100p or GBP £1.00). You then have to decide whether to pay 10p to reduce the other participant's income by half (50p).

By paying 10 pence, you can reduce the other partner's income by 50 pence (£0.50). The other participant makes the same decision in regard to your income concurrently. She/He can choose between leaving your income unaltered, or paying 10p to reduce it by half to 50 pence (£0.50).

If both of you choose to leave the other person's income unaltered, both of you will earn the 100 pence (GBP £1.00). If both of you choose to pay to reduce the other person's income, both of you will earn 40 pence (£0.50) (100p-50p-10p).

If you choose to reduce the other participant's income, but he/she decides to leave your income unaltered, you will earn 90 pence (GBP £0.90) and the other participant will earn 50 pence (£0.50).

If you choose not to reduce the other participant's income, but he/she decides to reduce your income, you will earn 50 pence (£0.50) and the other participant will earn 90 pence (GBP £0.90).

After you and the other participant have decided whether or not to reduce the other person’s income, a virtual throw of a dice makes the final decision whether your or the other participant’s income is reduced. This is done in order to hide your decisions from each other – if your income is reduced you’ll never know whether this was due to the other participant’s decision or the dice roll in the live trials. You will see 4 practice or demonstration trials in which the dice roll is reported to help you understand clearly how the experiment works.

The dice will be “thrown” twice. Once for you and another time for the other participant. Although the dice is virtual, the probabilities of rolling any number are identical to those in the real-world and each roll outcome is random and independent of all other rolls. You will not see the dice roll.

If the die shows 1 or 6 your income or the income of the other participant (depending for whom the dice has been thrown) will be reduced. This reduction is independent of your or the other participants’ decision. If the dice shows any other number (2,3,4,5) then your or the other participant’s decision will be realized: If you and/or the other participant have decided to reduce the other person’s income – the income will be reduced. If you and/or the other participant have decided not to reduce the other person’s income – the income will not be reduced.

Therefore, if your income or the other participant’s income is reduced by 50 pence (£0.50), you will never learn what the reason for this reduction has been: the decision of the partner or the results of the throw of the die. Similarly if their income is reduced they will never know whether this was because the dice rolled a 1 or 6 or because you decided to pay to reduce their income and the dice rolled a 2, 3, 4, or 5, implementing your decision.

You will complete 4 practice trials in which the other participant’s decisions will be simulated and feedback will be immediate – thus you will see how your decisions affect the other participant and how their decisions affect you. This is just a demonstration, no money is involved at this point and you will be told what the dice roll number was, the simulated decision of the other participant and how much you and they would have received if this practice round was a live trial. After the practice trials, you will be notified that the live trails have begun. You will receive no feedback in each of the 10 live rounds of the task. This can make the decision-making repetitive. We ask that you remember that your decisions will affect the income of another participant, just as theirs will affect yours and therefore we ask you to continue to consider each decision closely.
